Wolner Receivership Battle Precedents Florida 5th DCA & Circuit precedents

Forensic Context: In Plantation of Leesburg HOA v. Wolner, receiver fees reached ~$46,000 to collect an underlying $480 assessment delinquency. The resident was hospitalized. Florida courts heavily scrutinize receiver appointments, proportionality of collection fees, and homestead rights.

Florida Legal Standard: Courts will vacate receiver appointments where the underlying debt is trivial, the process lacks proper notice, or corporate actions represent bad faith. Homestead protections under the Florida Constitution prohibit forced sales or assessments that circumvent standard protections.

Vulnerability Index HIGH
Primary Statutes F.S. 720.3085, F.S. 720.311
Key Issue Proportionality & Homestead Safeguards

Special Assessment Posting & Notice F.S. 720.309 / 5th DCA rulings

Forensic Context: The 2021 ($1,260/household) and 2023 ($225/lot) special assessments are under suit. Plaintiffs allege procedural failures: lack of physical 48-hour board-meeting postings specifying assessments and failure to reach quorums.

Florida Legal Standard: Strict compliance is mandatory for special assessments. Rulings state that failure to physically post notice of meetings with assessment details at least 48 hours in advance renders the assessment a legal nullity. Re-votes do not retroactively cure past invalid assessments.

Vulnerability Index CATASTROPHIC
Primary Statutes F.S. 720.303(2)(c)2, F.S. 720.309
Key Issue Conspicuous Physical Meeting Notice

Golf Course LLC Separateness & Veil-Piercing Dania Jai-Alai Palace, Inc. v. SY. Assocs.

Forensic Context: The board maintains that residential master funds are shielded because golf course debt resides in a separate LLC. However, the HOA has funded consistent $42,300/month Special Activity draws from residential assessments to the LLC.

Florida Legal Standard: Under Florida corporate law, the "corporate shield" is pierced if the parent entity exercises complete dominion, commingles assets, shares directors with zero conflict procedures, or uses the subsidiary as a mere shell. If pierced, the HOA master funds are fully exposed to golf liabilities.

The Golden Tagline Trap — A Cautionary Tale of Green Fairways and Gray Hair

1988. A team of real estate marketers standing on a sunny plot of Central Florida dirt scribbled down a tagline that would eventually lock thousands of future retirees into a multi-million-dollar economic puzzle: “More Golf, More Friends, More Fun.” For nearly four decades, that slogan painted an idyllic retirement—a 2,820-home paradise of manicured fairways and cold drinks at Steve’s Bar & Grill. But beneath the turf of the Otter Creek and Cranes Roost courses lay an expensive truth.

Act I — The Illusion of the Cheap Paradise. Year after year, HOA dues stayed artificially low. The community’s capital reserve fund silently deteriorated to a terrifying 12% adequacy level. They were driving a luxury car on an empty tank. Then the private golf operator looked at red ink and threatened to walk away.

Act II — The Golden Ransom. If those 36 holes went dark or were sold for high-density development, baseline property values for all 2,820 homes would plummet. In October 2021, the HOA levied a mandatory $1,260 per household special assessment, raising millions to buy out the private golf operator and take over Steve’s Bar & Grill. They formed a Golf Course LLC—but independent legal reviews later found overlapping master board agreements left residential assets fully exposed. The homeowners had accidentally become owners of a struggling country club.

Act III — The Bill Comes Due. By 2023, another $225 per lot assessment funded golf-course renovations. On September 5, 2024, an organized group of homeowners sued their own HOA over broken statutory notice protocols; Florida law now required active lawsuits to be disclosed to buyers, slowing sales. A botched receivership over a $480 delinquency snowballed into $46,000 in receiver fees and national news coverage of HOA overreach.

Act IV — The Crossroads of 2026. Monthly dues rose $30 just to stay operational. Internal bad debt hit $222,000, with half tied to 57 severely delinquent accounts. The board proposed a $990 Capital Contribution Fee on incoming buyers—but distrust ran so deep it failed by just 41 votes. The community had voted against its own rescue.

Ending 1 — Heads in the Sand: Fatigue wins. Low turnout returns the entrenched board. Hurricane Milton cleanup crashes through remaining accounts. A $2,500 emergency special assessment hits every home. Delinquencies surge past $500,000. Property values drop 20%. The front gate still says “More Golf, More Friends, More Fun” while the fairways grow patchy and the clubhouse roof wears blue tarps.

Ending 2 — The Grassroots Awakening: The 41-vote loss becomes the alarm bell. Fifteen Block Captains deploy a statutory, data-driven campaign. A historic December election sweeps in a new board. They execute three revenue plays: a solar lease on a 50-acre interior buffer, a conservation easement converting a defunct back nine into an eco-park, and single-account unification merging HOA and Golf LLC funds. External revenues wipe out bad debt. Reserves surge from 12% to fully funded. Property values climb. By year-end, residents gather at a packed, profitable Steve’s Bar & Grill—having finally delivered, through hard work and engagement, the promise on the front gate from 1988.

History of The Plantation

Here is the strictly factual, chronological history of The Plantation at Leesburg, including how and when it was advertised as a golf community, based only on verified public records and established facts:

1985: Land Acquisition

  • The Facts: Lakewood Development Co. purchases approximately 999 acres of land in Leesburg, Florida, to develop a 55+ active adult community.
  • The Advertising: At this stage, there is no public advertising, but the foundational master plan is established around a golf-centric layout.

1987: Development Begins

  • The Facts: Pringle Development begins construction on the site, building the infrastructure, residential lots, and the first golf course.
  • The Advertising: Early conceptual marketing and pre-sales materials are used to sell the idea of a golf-focused lifestyle to secure initial buyers before the physical community is complete.

1988: Otter Creek Opens and Initial Marketing

  • The Facts: The first 18-hole golf course, Otter Creek, opens.
  • The Advertising: The community officially launches with a major print media campaign, including advertisements in the Orlando Sentinel. The explicit tagline used in these 1988 advertisements is “More Golf, More Friends, More Fun.” The word “Golf” is placed first, explicitly selling the public on the premise that this is a premier golf destination.

1996: Cranes Roost Opens

  • The Facts: The second 18-hole golf course, Cranes Roost, opens, expanding the community’s golf footprint to 36 holes.
  • The Advertising: The community is re-marketed to a new wave of buyers. Advertising shifts to promote The Plantation as a “36-hole golf destination,” elevating the prestige of the neighborhood and attracting new buyers to the lots bordering the second course.

The Transition: Course Acquisition and Corporate Structure

  • The Facts: At a later date, the residents agreed to purchase both 18-hole golf courses. The courses were placed under a separate legal entity, “The Plantation at Leesburg Golf Course, LLC.” The LLC manages its own finances and assessments, which are separate from the main HOA dues (golf is explicitly excluded from standard HOA dues). The physical maintenance of the courses is contracted out to a third party, BrightView Golf Maintenance.
  • The Advertising: The external marketing continues to rely heavily on the golf lifestyle, while internally, the community must navigate the financial and operational realities of the newly acquired Golf Course LLC.

Present Day: Current Status

  • The Facts: The community consists of exactly 2,820 single-family homes.
  • The Advertising: Despite internal financial discussions regarding the upkeep of the courses, external advertising remains unchanged from the developer days. Real estate agents and brokerages continue to heavily market The Plantation at Leesburg to outside buyers as an “award-winning gated golf community.” The original 1988 promise of “More Golf” is still used to sell homes today, even as the current residents manage the ongoing operational and financial realities of maintaining two 18-hole courses.
