2. Detailed Option Analysis
Option A: Consolidate to Cranes Roost; Convert Otter Creek to Private Resident Park
* Description: Retain Cranes Roost as the community's singular 18-hole championship course. Close Otter Creek (which has severe flooding issues) and convert its fairways into a low-maintenance, private resident park (nature trails, picnic nodes, and wetland observation boardwalks).
* Feasibility: High. Requires a member vote to amend the Declaration of Covenants.
* Upfront Capital: $40,000 (turf seeding, trail markers, legal fees).
* Annual Operating Cost: $445,000/year (Cranes Roost maintenance + park upkeep).
* Financial Result: Saves $705,000/year vs. status quo. The existing $15/month assessment base ($507,600/year) completely covers operations, leaving a $62,600/year surplus to rebuild Cranes Roost reserves.

Option B: Keep Cranes Roost + Convert Otter Creek to Private Par-3 & Range
* Description: Retain Cranes Roost. Convert Otter Creek's higher-elevation, non-flooding areas into a private 9-hole short course and driving range, while dedicating the flood-prone sections to water retention.
* Feasibility: High on zoning. Medium on operations.
* Upfront Capital: $240,000 (tees, greens, picker vehicle, dispenser machine).
* Annual Operating Cost: $525,000/year (net of range card revenue).
* Financial Result: Saves $515,000/year vs. status quo, but leaves a small $17,400/year deficit over the golf assessment base.

Option C: Keep Cranes Roost + Private Mixed-Use Recreation (Disc Golf & Trails)
* Description: Retain Cranes Roost. Repurpose Otter Creek into a private resident-only 18-hole disc golf course, walking trails, and pickleball courts.
* Feasibility: High on zoning. Requires a member vote to amend covenants to install non-golf recreation on platted golf land.
* Upfront Capital: $470,000 (pickleball courts, disc golf, paved trails).
* Annual Operating Cost: $515,000/year (Cranes Roost maintenance + park upkeep).
* Financial Result: Saves $635,000/year vs. status quo. Creates a minor $7,400/year deficit over the golf assessment base.

Option D: Lease Both Courses to a Professional Golf Management Company
* Description: Lease operations of both courses to a professional third-party management firm (e.g., Troon, KemperSports). The operator takes over all staffing and turf upkeep.
* Feasibility: Low-Medium. The operator will demand public play access and that the HOA replace the failing Cranes Roost irrigation system.
* Upfront Capital: $1,560,000 (irrigation replacement + legal/RFP costs).
* Annual Operating Cost: $220,000/year (management fee/subsidy + irrigation loan service).
* Financial Result: Saves $930,000/year on operations. Generates a $287,600/year surplus under the golf assessment, but saddles the HOA with $1.5M in capital debt.

Option E: Keep Both Courses Operational (36-Hole "Status Quo" Capital Recovery Plan)
* Description: Retain and run both courses as active 18-hole courses by funding the backlog of infrastructure repairs and adjusting the dedicated golf assessment to cover actual operating losses and new capital debt.
* Feasibility: Very Low. Homeowners must approve a $2.55M bank loan, an 87% increase in golf dues, and opening the community to public play to drive play from 32K up to the 56,000 rounds/year break-even threshold.
* Upfront Capital: $2,550,000 (Cranes Roost irrigation replacement + Otter Creek drainage overhaul).
* Annual Operating Cost: $1,396,000/year (operations + cart shop + debt service on new CapEx loan).
* Financial Result: Net annual HOA cost of $1,046,000/year (net of $350K projected public play revenue). Hikes overall dues to $200.50/month.

3. Audited Dues Hike Calculation (Option E Details)
To keep both courses operational under HOA management, the dedicated golf assessment must increase from $15.00/month to $35.50/month (an increase of $20.50/month).
* Monthly Increase: An additional $20.50/month per home.
* Total Dues Bill: Total monthly HOA dues will increase from $180.00/month to $200.50/month per home.
* Overall Dues Hike: This is an 11.4% increase in the overall monthly HOA dues paid by each household.
* Annual Impact: Increases the total annual HOA cost per home from $2,160.00/year ($180 * 12) to $2,406.00/year ($200.50 * 12)—a net annual increase of $246.00/year per household.

Math Breakdown
A. Baseline Operating Cost (No CapEx)
* YTD April 2025 Actual Loss: $149,457.86 over 4 months, which averages $37,364.47/month.
* HOA Transfers: This loss was in addition to the regular monthly transfer of $42,300/month.
* True Baseline Operating Cost: $42,300 + $37,364.47 = $79,664.47/month.
* Per-Home Operating Dues: $79,664.47 / 2,820 = $28.25/month per home.
B. Infrastructure Debt Service (CapEx)
* New Loan Requirement: $2,550,000 bank loan including $50K design/permitting.
* Annual Debt Service: $246,000/year, or $20,500/month.
* Per-Home Debt Dues: $20,500 / 2,820 = $7.27/month per home.
C. Combined Total
* Total Monthly Assessment Required: $28.25 (Operating Baseline) + $7.27 (CapEx Debt) = $35.52/month per home.
* Final Dedicated Golf Fee: Rounded to $35.50/month per home.
* Net Increase: Hikes dedicated golf fees from $15.00 to $35.50/month (an increase of $20.50/month).

History of The Plantation

Here is the strictly factual, chronological history of The Plantation at Leesburg, including how and when it was advertised as a golf community, based only on verified public records and established facts:

1985: Land Acquisition

  • The Facts: Lakewood Development Co. purchases approximately 999 acres of land in Leesburg, Florida, to develop a 55+ active adult community.
  • The Advertising: At this stage, there is no public advertising, but the foundational master plan is established around a golf-centric layout.

1987: Development Begins

  • The Facts: Pringle Development begins construction on the site, building the infrastructure, residential lots, and the first golf course.
  • The Advertising: Early conceptual marketing and pre-sales materials are used to sell the idea of a golf-focused lifestyle to secure initial buyers before the physical community is complete.

1988: Otter Creek Opens and Initial Marketing

  • The Facts: The first 18-hole golf course, Otter Creek, opens.
  • The Advertising: The community officially launches with a major print media campaign, including advertisements in the Orlando Sentinel. The explicit tagline used in these 1988 advertisements is “More Golf, More Friends, More Fun.” The word “Golf” is placed first, explicitly selling the public on the premise that this is a premier golf destination.

1996: Cranes Roost Opens

  • The Facts: The second 18-hole golf course, Cranes Roost, opens, expanding the community’s golf footprint to 36 holes.
  • The Advertising: The community is re-marketed to a new wave of buyers. Advertising shifts to promote The Plantation as a “36-hole golf destination,” elevating the prestige of the neighborhood and attracting new buyers to the lots bordering the second course.

The Transition: Course Acquisition and Corporate Structure

  • The Facts: At a later date, the residents agreed to purchase both 18-hole golf courses. The courses were placed under a separate legal entity, “The Plantation at Leesburg Golf Course, LLC.” The LLC manages its own finances and assessments, which are separate from the main HOA dues (golf is explicitly excluded from standard HOA dues). The physical maintenance of the courses is contracted out to a third party, BrightView Golf Maintenance.
  • The Advertising: The external marketing continues to rely heavily on the golf lifestyle, while internally, the community must navigate the financial and operational realities of the newly acquired Golf Course LLC.

Present Day: Current Status

  • The Facts: The community consists of exactly 2,820 single-family homes.
  • The Advertising: Despite internal financial discussions regarding the upkeep of the courses, external advertising remains unchanged from the developer days. Real estate agents and brokerages continue to heavily market The Plantation at Leesburg to outside buyers as an “award-winning gated golf community.” The original 1988 promise of “More Golf” is still used to sell homes today, even as the current residents manage the ongoing operational and financial realities of maintaining two 18-hole courses.
